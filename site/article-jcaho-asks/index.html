What a JCAHO surveyor actually asks for.

Three years of hospital audits, distilled into the five evidence artifacts surveyors request first. Spoiler: none of them are the spreadsheet your vendor prepared.

By the IQS Research Team Apr 09, 2026 7 min read

Every JCAHO survey I have ever sat through, and there have been quite a few, starts the same way. The surveyor walks onto the unit, introduces themselves, and then spends roughly forty minutes asking for five specific things. The rest of the week is built on whether those five things are ready. If they are, the survey proceeds at the pace the surveyor chooses. If they are not, the survey proceeds at the pace your team can scramble, which is the worse pace to survey at.

Every EVS contractor I have worked with has offered to "prepare" for these surveys by producing a quality binder, a run-rate spreadsheet, or a dashboard PDF. I have never once seen a surveyor ask for a quality binder. I have never once seen a surveyor open a run-rate spreadsheet. I have never once seen a surveyor look at a dashboard PDF for longer than it took to be polite. They ask for the same five artifacts, and those artifacts are not what most vendors are producing. Here they are, in rough order of request frequency.

1. The tracer, not the report.

Surveyors do tracers. They pick a patient, a room, or a piece of equipment, and they follow it through the institution. "Show me this soiled utility room. Show me the last twelve cleaning events for this soiled utility room. Show me the evidence — photo, signature, timestamp — for each event." They are not asking for a summary. They are asking for a chain. If your vendor's system aggregates before it logs, you do not have a chain. You have a report, and the surveyor will not accept it.

What to have ready

A per-location event log with one row per cleaning event, linked to the inspector's identity, the timestamp, and at least one evidentiary artifact. Twelve events deep, minimum. Searchable by room number and by date.

2. The exception, not the pass rate.

The surveyor will ask you about the last time a clean failed. If your answer is "our pass rate is 98%," the surveyor will not ask you again. They will ask the nurse manager, and the nurse manager will tell them about the time the OR terminal clean was rejected. You want to be the source of that story. You want to bring up the exception yourself, describe what the vendor did about it, describe what you did about it, and describe why it is not likely to recur. Surveys do not want to know your pass rate. They want to know your failure process.

What to have ready

A list of the last ten inspection failures on the unit the surveyor is standing in, with the corrective-action narrative for each. Corrective-action narratives should be one paragraph, written by the crew lead involved, not by the vendor's account manager.

"I want to see the one you are least proud of. If you don't want to show me the one you are least proud of, I am going to find it on my own, and that will be much worse for both of us." JCAHO surveyor, verbatim, 2024 survey

3. The rubric, not the results.

The surveyor will ask to see the rubric your EVS contractor is graded against. They are looking for two things: whether the rubric reflects current JCAHO standards, and whether the rubric has been updated in the last eighteen months. A rubric that has not been updated in two years is, by default, not current. A rubric that was authored by the vendor rather than co-authored with your infection prevention team is, by default, suspect.

What to have ready

A PDF of the active rubric, with a revision history on the last page. Revision history should name the IP representative who approved each change.

4. The escalation path, in writing.

"If an EVS tech sees a potential IP event — say, a sharps container left unlocked — who do they tell, how quickly do they tell them, and how do you know they told them?" If your answer involves the word "should" rather than a specific role and specific time, the surveyor will not believe the path exists. They are asking whether your system has a documented, enforced mechanism by which a frontline observation reaches a clinical decision-maker.

What to have ready

A single-page flow diagram. "Observation → Crew lead (within 5 min) → Shift supervisor (within 15 min) → IP on-call (within 60 min)." Laminated, on every supervisor's clipboard. Tested, within the last quarter, with a drill.

5. The evidence, with timestamps.

This is the one where every facility I have worked with fails, and it is the one that IQS Flow exists to address. The surveyor will eventually, within the first morning, ask to see a photo. "Show me the last wipe-down of this soiled utility room." You will open whatever system your vendor uses. You will find a checklist entry. The checklist entry will have a timestamp. It will not have a photo.

The surveyor will not be cruel about this. They will simply note it, and they will stop trusting the checklist. Everything that happens in the rest of the survey will happen under the assumption that your evidence trail is incomplete. This is a worse position to survey from than almost any other.

5 of 5 artifacts surveyors request first, in order. None of them is a summary dashboard. Four of them require per-event evidence, not roll-ups.

What the binder does get you.

In the interest of being fair, there is one thing a good quality binder does. It signals to the surveyor that your team has a culture of documentation, even if the documentation itself is not what they will ultimately ask for. I have watched surveyors visibly relax when they see a well-organized binder, even when they end up looking at zero of its contents. This is not nothing. But it is not survey preparation. It is survey theater. If your vendor is producing only the binder, you are going to be surprised on day one of your next survey, and the surprise will not be pleasant.

Prepare for what the surveyor will actually ask for. Ask your vendor to produce the five artifacts above, monthly, whether a survey is imminent or not. If they cannot produce them, you have learned something useful about your vendor well before JCAHO learns it for you.
