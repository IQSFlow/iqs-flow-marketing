The Feature · Vol 04 Issue 11
A study of 41,227 re-scored inspections found vendor-reported quality runs 11.3 points over independently measured quality. We looked at where the gap comes from.

The eleven-point three gap.

11.3
Fig. 01 — Mean delta, vendor score vs IQS score, n=41,227 Illustration: N. Eshun

Every operations leader we interviewed could tell us exactly when they stopped trusting their monthly quality report. It was always a specific Monday, always a specific finding, always a moment where the report said 98% and the reality said otherwise. We wanted to know if that gap was real in aggregate, or just in memory.

So over eighteen months we re-scored 41,227 inspections across three industries — aviation ground services, acute-care environmental services, and multi-tenant commercial cleaning — using the same rubric the vendor had used, run by an independent inspector. The deltas were remarkably stable. In every industry, across every framework we tested, the vendor's self-reported score ran hot by a similar amount.

The mean was 11.3 points. The median was 10.8. And critically: the variance by site was low. This isn't a story about a handful of bad vendors dragging an average around. It's structural. It reflects how inspection rubrics interact with the humans who have to grade against them when those humans are also the ones being graded.

The interesting question isn't how big the gap is. It's what the gap is made of, and why rubric design matters more than crew quality for closing it.
