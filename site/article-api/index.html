Product · Shipping

The API we didn’t want to ship, and why we shipped it anyway.

Customer operations platforms were asking for event streams. We had reservations. Here is the shape of the REST-plus-webhooks v2 spec that went out, what we cut, and the three things we will not expose over the API no matter who asks.

By Joshua Hinton Feb 07, 2026 3 min read

We shipped a public API in late 2025. We spent about eight months arguing about whether to. This is a short piece about what we shipped, what we refused to ship, and the reasoning behind both.

Why the reservations.

IQS Flow produces a record that three commercial parties (customer, vendor, inspector) have agreed to treat as the source of truth for a contract. An API that exposes that record creates two risks. First, a third-party system that writes scores into our database by API could undermine the independence claim that the whole product stands on. Second, a third-party system that reads partial records could produce derived analytics that attribute opinions to us we did not actually form. The first risk is the serious one.

We were hearing the request from customer operations platforms (“integrated workplace management systems,” mostly) that wanted to pull our scores into their dashboards. The request was reasonable. The worry was the slippery slope from “pull our scores” to “let us write scores back” to “let us reconcile disagreements in our system before yours sees them.” That last step is the one that breaks the product.

What shipped.

The v2 API surface is read-mostly. Five endpoints, plus webhooks:

  • GET /inspections — list scored inspections, paginated, scoped to the authenticating customer’s tenant and rubrics.
  • GET /scores/:id — one score, with the five-dimension VAS breakdown.
  • GET /evidence/:id — signed URL to a photo or GPS artifact, 15-minute expiry, audit-logged on access.
  • POST /webhooks — subscribe to score, evidence, or deviation events.
  • GET /vendors/:id/vas — current VAS plus trailing 12 months for one vendor.

Auth is OAuth 2 client-credentials, scoped to the customer’s tenant. Rate limits are generous (120 req/min sustained, 300 burst) because the typical integration reads on a five-minute cadence. The webhook signing is HMAC-SHA256 with a rotating secret on a 90-day window.

3 Endpoints we will not ship, regardless of who asks. Writing scores is one of them.

What doesn’t ship.

Three endpoints that we were asked for, repeatedly, and that we did not ship. Each has a reason.

POST /scores. The single most common request, and the one we are most certain about. If a third-party system can write a score, the score is no longer an independent measurement. We are happy to let customers annotate a score with context (“weather delay,” “VIP event”) via a separate notes endpoint, which does ship. We are not willing to let them assert a grade. That is the inspector’s job, and the inspector’s identity sits in our system and nowhere else.

PATCH /inspections/:id. Once an inspection has been scored and signed, it becomes part of an audit trail that other parties depend on. Letting a third-party system edit a scored inspection breaks the immutability guarantee. If a score is wrong, the remediation is a re-inspection, which produces a new scored record that references the original. That is slower than an edit, and it is the right kind of slower.

GET /inspectors/:id/schedule. Inspector independence depends, in part, on vendors not knowing in advance which site an inspector will visit on a given day. We publish completed inspections, with inspector identity, after the fact. We do not publish upcoming schedules. This was a surprise to some customer ops teams that wanted to “coordinate” inspections with their own shift changes; politely, no.

What we learned in the first 90 days.

Three small things worth mentioning, because they did not make it into the API documentation and we should probably fix that.

First, most integrations read from GET /inspections with a ?since= query parameter, on a five-minute cadence. This is fine, but it is wasteful. We are going to nudge integrators toward webhooks in the next round of documentation. The cadence polling is producing about 180x the API traffic that the equivalent webhook subscription would.

Second, the evidence URL’s 15-minute expiry is, empirically, too short for customer systems that want to embed images in a dashboard that users load from cache. We are extending to 4 hours for a specific scope=embed flag, with a reduced-resolution image and a separate audit log.

Third, the VAS endpoint gets queried more than any other. That is probably a signal about what customer ops platforms actually want to surface. We are considering a leaner /summary endpoint that returns just the headline VAS and a trend arrow, for use in ticker widgets.

The philosophical part.

We think of the public API as a promise to the three parties in the contract, not to the developer writing against it. The customer’s ops platform wants to integrate; that is reasonable. The vendor wants to integrate; also reasonable. The inspector wants their work not to be interfered with; that is the line we are defending. An API that produces integration at the cost of inspector independence is an API we would rather not ship. The version we did ship is what we could defend.

Full spec at iqsflow.com/api. We would rather have fewer integrations that respect the product’s theory than many integrations that erode it.
