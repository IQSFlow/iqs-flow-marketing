Compliance · Framework

ATA 103 is not a checklist. It is a cabin standard.

Aviation ops teams sometimes read ATA 103 as a pass/fail inspection list. The document itself is written as an intent standard, and the gap between those two readings produces most of the audit findings we see at hub stations. Here is how we translate intent into a 22-item rubric that an airline QA team will sign off on.

By the IQS Research Team Feb 16, 2026 6 min read

Air Transport Association Specification 103 is, nominally, the standard governing aviation fueling cleanliness and quality. In aviation cabin services, a parallel family of airline-specific standards sits on top of it: ATA 104, branded contracts, IOSA audit items, and so on. What unites them all is that they are written as intent documents. They say things like “the cabin shall be presentable,” “the lavatories shall be hygienic and operational,” “amenity placement shall be consistent with brand specification.” These are not inspection items. They are outcomes a reasonable airline QA team expects at the end of a turn.

The error most operations teams make is to treat these intent clauses as if they were directly inspectable. A ramp lead reads “cabin shall be presentable” and drafts a rubric line that says “cabin presentable: yes/no.” That line is unworkable. Two inspectors will disagree on it. Crews cannot train against it. QA auditors cannot cite it in a finding. And the metric that emerges from it tells nobody anything useful.

The job of a cabin-quality rubric is to translate the intent language into a set of observable, weighted items that, taken together, produce the outcome the intent document describes. That translation is craft. It is also the part that an inspection software vendor cannot do for you; we can provide the rubric engine, but the rubric itself belongs to your operation, your audit history, and your contract.

Three intent buckets.

In our experience, ATA 103 and its cousin standards decompose cleanly into three intent buckets. This is not in the standard itself; it is an organizing scheme we have found useful across the nine hub-station operations we work with.

Passenger-facing. These are intent clauses about what a passenger sees and touches. Cabin surfaces, seat-back pockets, tray tables, galley appearance, lavatory condition. The inspection items here should be weighted most heavily, because they are the ones a non-aviation auditor (a surveyor, a regulator, a reporter with a smartphone) will read as “clean” or “dirty” on the first glance.

Safety-critical. These are intent clauses about escape-path clarity, placard legibility, emergency-equipment visibility. Safety-critical items are usually small in number (3–4 per rubric) but carry an absolute-pass requirement: a single fail here escalates. Do not weight these numerically; treat them as gating.

Contractual. These are intent clauses about brand specification, amenity placement, magazine presentation, scent profile. They are real obligations that appear in the airline’s contract with the ground-service provider. They are not safety items, and they are not the most visible-to-passenger items, but they are the ones the airline’s commercial team cares about at contract review.

The 22-item rubric.

Across the hub stations we work with, the rubric that consistently holds up to an airline QA audit has 22 items. This number is not mystical; it is the point at which the three intent buckets are each represented by enough items to produce a stable score, without the rubric getting so long that turn-time pressure makes it impossible to run. (The “Writing a rubric your night shift will actually run” piece in this issue makes the longer argument about why more than about 28 items breaks.)

What goes wrong.

Two common failure modes, neither of them the fault of the crews running the rubric.

The first is literal readings. A station ops team reads ATA 103 verbatim and ends up with a 60-item rubric full of near-duplicates. Every one of the 60 items is defensible as a line-for-line reading of the standard. Taken together, they produce a rubric that is un-runnable on a 38-minute turn. The crew starts skipping, the scores become fiction, and the airline’s QA audit finds the gap within two months.

The second is contractual creep. The airline’s commercial team adds items to the rubric each time a passenger complaint comes in. Over eighteen months, the contractual bucket swells from five items to nineteen. The items are each reasonable individually. Collectively, they crowd out the safety-critical and passenger-facing buckets. We have audited rubrics where 42% of the weight sat on brand-compliance items and only 31% sat on lavatory and galley.

A small, unromantic observation.

The airlines whose cabin rubrics work are the ones with a single QA engineer who owns the rubric and defends it from both kinds of creep. That person’s job is, specifically, to say no. No to literal readings. No to adding an item every time a passenger complains on social media. No to a contractual team that wants brand-compliance over hygiene. The rubric is a contract; the QA engineer is its lawyer. Operations that do not have this person end up with a rubric that nobody quite owns, and that tends to drift in whichever direction the most recent complaint came from.

If you are standing up a new station-operations rubric against ATA 103 or an airline-specific cabin standard, find that person first. The tool comes second.
