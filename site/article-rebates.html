Field Notes · The Case

The crew that quietly saved $1.4M in rebates.

A real-estate portfolio restructured twelve janitorial contracts around independent ISSA scoring. Rebates re-priced at renewal. Not one vendor argued. Nothing about the crews changed. The accountability did.

By Venice Collier Mar 13, 2026 6 min read

A REIT I will call Meridian runs a thirty-building downtown portfolio across two cities. Their janitorial contracts, twelve of them, had been written over the course of about a decade by three different procurement leads, and they had the shape of contracts written over ten years by three different procurement leads. Different SLAs. Different inspection cadences. Different rubrics. Different rebate structures. One of the contracts was, embarrassingly, on a paper rider from 2013 that nobody could find the digital copy of.

When Meridian engaged us, they were not trying to save money on cleaning. They were trying to standardize. The money question arrived by accident, and it arrived because of one specific change we made in the first ninety days: we put every building on the same inspection rubric, scored by the same independent crew, reported into a single system. We did not change a single cleaning vendor. We did not change a single cleaning crew. We did not change a single scope of work. We changed only how the work was measured. That is the entire intervention.

The spread.

On a unified rubric, the thirty buildings spread out. The spread was wider than anyone at Meridian had expected. The best building in the portfolio scored a 92.4 over the first quarter. The worst scored a 71.1. Median was 84. Across twelve vendors, the vendor-to-vendor variance was almost as wide as the building-to-building variance, which told us something useful: the spread was not explained by building age, tenant mix, or square footage. It was explained by who was actually cleaning.

21.3 pts Spread between best and worst buildings in the portfolio, on the same unified rubric. Before, every vendor reported themselves above 90.

The rebates that were not being collected.

Here is where it gets interesting. Seven of the twelve contracts had performance-based rebate clauses. Rebates were meant to trigger when vendor-reported score dropped below 85. In the four years leading up to our engagement, none of the seven contracts had triggered a single rebate. Vendor-reported scores had never dipped below 90. The clauses were, functionally, decorative.

Under independent scoring, six of those seven contracts were under 85 in the first quarter. Meridian's legal team reviewed the clauses. The contracts required "measured score" — not "vendor-reported score" — to be below threshold. The contracts did not specify who had to do the measuring. Meridian had always assumed the vendor's own measurement was the measurement. Legal read the contracts again, with fresh eyes, and said: "no, the independent score qualifies."

Meridian sent twelve letters. The letters enumerated the buildings that had scored below threshold, the rebate percentages owed, and the total amounts. The total across six contracts was $1.4 million, against about $14 million in annualized spend. Meridian offered the vendors the option to accept the rebate retroactively, or to renegotiate the rubric and thresholds going forward. Eleven of the twelve vendors chose to renegotiate. One paid the rebate and renewed anyway.

Nobody argued.

This is the part I find worth writing about. The vendors did not argue. There were, over the following six weeks, quite a few conversations. There were a few requests for methodology detail, which we supplied. There was one request to re-score a specific building, which we did, and the re-score came back within 0.4 points of the original. There was a lot of discussion about what thresholds should look like going forward.

But no vendor disputed a number. Not one.

"We've been saying for years that our scores were higher than our competitors'. We believed it. We submitted scorecards that said so. When an independent inspector walked the same buildings with the same rubric, our scores were exactly average. I wasn't angry about the rebate. I was embarrassed about the four years before the rebate." Regional VP, national janitorial contractor, verbatim

The crews did not change. The scopes of work did not change. The cleaning itself was unchanged during this period and scored nearly identically in Q2 as it had in Q1. What changed was that both the customer and the vendor were now working from the same number. And the presence of a shared number, produced by someone neither side controlled, made the negotiation possible in a way that vendor-reported numbers never had.

Two things this is not.

I want to be careful about what this case study claims, because the easy read of it is "fire your vendors and save fifteen percent of your cleaning spend." That is not what happened, and it is not what we would recommend.

First, the $1.4M was money that had been contractually owed for years and not collected. It was not a cost reduction. It was the recovery of a structural under-invoicing caused by the customer using the vendor's own measurement to trigger customer-side rebates. This is a foreseeable and fixable design error in the contracts; it is not a claim that vendors should be paid less.

Second, the outcome was not adversarial. Eleven of twelve vendors renegotiated their contracts with Meridian. The renegotiated scopes were, in most cases, slightly larger and slightly better-paid than the originals, with clearer thresholds and fewer decorative clauses. Meridian's total cleaning spend went up, not down, once the renegotiation completed. What went down was the amount Meridian was paying for work that was not being performed at the standard the contract specified.

What to ask for Monday morning.

If you run a portfolio with performance-based vendor contracts, the question worth asking this week is: who, specifically, produces the number that triggers our rebate clauses? If the answer is "the vendor being rebated," you have found a small piece of structural leverage. It may be worth a phone call with your legal team.
