Product · The Rebuild

Four portals, one data model, the design principle behind our rebuild.

We spent nine months rebuilding four separate role portals on a single normalized data schema. Here is why that matters when you are negotiating vendor contracts, and how one shared source of truth prevents a failure mode we are calling “version drift.”

By Joshua Hinton Mar 26, 2026 4 min read

Before the rebuild, IQS Flow was four products sharing a logo. A customer portal that ops leads used to look at scores. A vendor portal that janitorial crews used to log work. An inspector portal that independent auditors used to enter re-scores. An admin portal that our internal team used to configure rubrics and onboard sites. Each had grown up at a slightly different moment in the company’s life. Each had, over time, developed its own slightly different vocabulary for the same underlying object. A “building” in the customer portal was a “site” in the vendor portal was a “venue” in the inspector portal. The schemas had drifted. The data had drifted with them.

We spent about nine months rebuilding all four portals on a single normalized schema. I want to be honest about why we did that, because the usual engineering-blog answer (“velocity”, “consistency”) is not the real answer. The real answer is about what the product is for.

What the product is for.

IQS Flow exists to produce a single number that all three commercial parties in a facility-cleaning contract (the customer, the vendor, and the independent inspector) can agree is the score. Not a number the customer believes. Not a number the vendor can defend. The number. The product’s usefulness depends on the three parties looking at the same record and recognizing it as the same record. The moment the customer sees a score of 87.4 and the vendor sees a score of 88.1 for the same inspection because one portal is reading from a cached ETL and the other is reading from a live OLAP, we have broken the thing we are selling.

That failure mode has a name in our engineering rituals now. We call it version drift, and the rebuild was motivated by a specific incident in late 2024 when a customer’s rebate conversation with their vendor stalled for four days because the two parties were looking at different numbers that were both technically correct within their respective systems. I will not go into the forensic detail of that incident here; a version of it is on our engineering blog. But the lesson out of it was sharp: a product that produces trust between commercial parties cannot also produce disagreement between itself and itself.

0 Synchronization jobs in the new architecture. Four portals, one database, no ETL. The drift failure mode is architecturally impossible.

The design principle.

The rebuild turned on one principle, which I can state in a sentence: the four portals are not four products, they are four scoped views of one record. The customer portal does not maintain its own copy of an inspection score. Neither does the vendor portal. There is one row in one table, and the four portals are four different permission-scoped, role-shaped lenses onto that one row. If the score updates, all four lenses update. There is no batch job. There is no cache layer. There is no reconciliation service. The architectural primitive is the lens, not the copy.

I want to be clear that this is not a novel idea. It is how most well-designed multi-tenant SaaS products already work. What made it hard for us was that we had grown past the point where the rebuild was trivial. We had seven years of customer data in four different shapes, and we needed to collapse that into one shape without losing a single inspection, scoring event, or evidence artifact. The migration took five months. The rebuild of the four portals on top of the new schema took another four.

What it changes for customers.

Three things are different now, and the third one is the one I care about.

First, role onboarding is faster. A new customer-side ops lead and a new vendor-side field manager used to require two configuration paths. They now share one. A new user is a record with a role attribute, not a record in a role-specific table. This is an internal improvement but a real one; it compressed new-user provisioning from about ninety minutes to about three.

Second, reporting is consistent. Every dashboard, every export, every API response now comes from the same query surface. A monthly scorecard the customer downloads is a query against the same rows the vendor’s field manager sees in real time. There is no “reporting delay,” because there is no reporting pipeline. Reports are queries.

Third, and this is the one I care about: the commercial conversation between customer and vendor has a single shared artifact. When a customer sends a rebate letter against a threshold, the vendor cannot respond with “we see a different number in our system” because there is no “our system” to see a different number in. The system is the same system. This has closed a failure mode we used to see about once a quarter, where a commercial dispute would escalate because the two parties’ software disagreed before the parties disagreed.

What we had to give up.

The rebuild had costs that are worth stating honestly. We lost the ability to ship portal-specific features quickly, because every feature now has to be considered across four roles. A feature that makes the customer portal better is not shippable if it leaks data the vendor should not see, or if it requires a schema change that would break the inspector portal. The review cycle for even small features went up by about forty percent. Velocity is down. Correctness is up. We think that is the right trade, but it is a trade, not a free lunch.

We also lost the ability to offer the four portals as separate products. For a while there was an internal proposal to package the inspector portal as a standalone SaaS for third-party audit firms. The proposal is dead, because the inspector portal is not a standalone product in the new architecture; it is a view. Viability as a standalone depended on having a standalone database, which we have consciously chosen not to have. This is a real business-model constraint and we made peace with it.

What this is not.

I want to close by saying what this piece is not, because engineering write-ups like this one tend to get read as endorsements of an architectural style. This is not an endorsement. The single-schema approach is right for a product whose job is to produce consensus between parties with conflicting incentives. It would be the wrong architecture for a product whose job is to offer four fundamentally different workflows to four fundamentally different users. The architecture should serve the product’s theory of what it does. Ours now does.
