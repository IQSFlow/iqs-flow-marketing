Writing a rubric your night shift will actually run.

The difference between a rubric crews use and one they quietly avoid is, on average, eleven items. We went through five years of completion-rate data to figure out what to cut, what to weight, and how to keep the rubric honest without making it hostile.

By the IQS Research Team Feb 25, 2026 5 min read

If you want to know whether a rubric is going to survive its first six months in the field, count the items on it. That is not the only thing that matters, but it is the first thing, and in our data it is the most predictive single variable. Below about 28 items, most rubrics run at a completion rate north of 85%. Above about 39 items, completion rates collapse. Crews start skipping, backfilling, or simply hitting “acceptable” on the whole list without visiting the areas. The rubric stops being a measurement and starts being a ritual.

This piece walks through what we have learned from five years of looking at rubric completion data across about 1,100 commercial-cleaning sites. It is a practical piece, not a methodological one. The claim is simple: most rubrics are too long, and the path to a usable rubric runs through cutting, not through adding.

The 11-item gap.

The typical rubric we see a customer draft, before we push back, has 39–45 items. The typical rubric the same customer ends up running after three months is around 28. The gap is, on average, 11 items. What gets cut is consistent enough to list:

  • Redundant item-pairs. “Floor clean” and “floor free of debris.” “Dispensers stocked” and “paper supply full.” These are not two items; they are one item with a synonym.
  • Evidence-of-work items. “Cleaning log signed.” “Cart checked out.” These belong in the evidence layer, not the quality layer. If the work happened, the log signs itself.
  • Outcome proxies. “Trash receptacles aesthetically placed.” Nobody can grade this consistently. Cut it and trust the trash-receptacle item already in the rubric.
  • Rare-event items. “Exterior windows streak-free.” In most buildings this is inspected quarterly, not nightly. It does not belong on the nightly rubric.
11 Average items cut between a customer’s initial rubric draft and the rubric they end up running after three months.

Weight matters more than count.

Cutting items is necessary but not sufficient. The second most predictive variable in rubric design is weight distribution. A rubric where every item is weighted equally produces a specific failure mode: crews optimize for item count, not for impact. They complete 28 low-effort items and skip the one high-impact item, because mathematically the skipped item costs them one point and the rubric reads “27/28 complete.”

The rubrics that run cleanly are the ones where two or three items carry outsized weight. In a healthcare EVS rubric, that is usually the patient-room discharge clean and the restroom disinfection. In aviation cabin, it is the galley and the lavatories. In commercial cleaning, it is the restroom and the entrance. Weight those items at 3× or 4× the others, and the crew understands, without being told, where to spend their time.

What your night shift is telling you.

If you have access to your inspection completion data, run two queries. First, completion rate by item. Which items get skipped? The answers will cluster. Second, grade distribution by item. Which items receive a full “acceptable” grade more than 97% of the time? Those items are almost always either unobservable, trivially easy, or being graded dishonestly. All three are reasons to cut.

We have, in the past, counseled customers to talk to their crews about rubric length. It is usually a short conversation. The crew will tell you exactly which items are the ones they are checking without visiting. They will be embarrassed about it. They will also be right. Crews know, better than anyone in the building, which lines of a rubric are making the building cleaner and which are making the paperwork longer. The rubric is a contract between you and the crew; if your half of the contract is unreasonable, do not be surprised that their half goes unmet.

A short checklist, about checklists.

When we help a customer revise a rubric, we work through four questions. In order:

  1. Is this item observable from the doorway, or does it require entering and touching a surface? The second kind is expensive. Only include it if the item matters.
  2. Will two inspectors agree on the grade? If not, the item is ambiguous and will be graded inconsistently. Either sharpen it or cut it.
  3. Does the item appear on the rubric because a customer incident prompted it once, years ago? If yes, and the incident has not recurred, it is a relic. Consider quarterly inclusion instead of nightly.
  4. If this item scored zero, would a reasonable operations leader care? If the answer is “not really,” cut it. A rubric with items nobody cares about teaches the crew that items do not matter.

Most rubrics, worked through these four questions, come out shorter and sharper. The customer is usually a little nervous during the process. They feel like they are losing coverage. What they are actually losing is the performance of coverage. The rubric that runs is the rubric that measures.
