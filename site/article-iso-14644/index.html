Compliance · The Case

ISO 14644 drift: from sensor alert to batch disposition in 47 minutes.

A cleanroom client had a Grade B particulate event on a Tuesday. Forty-seven minutes later, QA had signed the batch disposition. Here is what happened, minute by minute, and why the janitorial crew’s evidence was the part of the chain that made it possible.

By Joshua Hinton Mar 21, 2026 8 min read

A mid-sized specialty-pharma company we work with operates a Grade B cleanroom suite at their formulation site in the Mid-Atlantic. Their particle counters are set to alert at 80% of the ISO 14644 limit, which for Grade B at rest is 3,520 particles per cubic meter at ≥ 0.5 µm. On a Tuesday in September, at 10:43 local time, one of those counters fired. The reading had climbed from a baseline of about 620 particles to a peak of 4,820 in a span of roughly twelve minutes. That is a classic drift signature. The kind of event that, under the old documentation regime, would have produced a two-week batch hold and a QA investigation that read like a detective novel.

At 11:30 the same morning, forty-seven minutes after the alert fired, QA had signed the disposition. The batch released. The event was closed. I want to walk through what happened in those forty-seven minutes, because the part that actually made the speed possible was not the sensor system and it was not the QA workflow. It was the janitorial evidence chain, which the client had previously considered a compliance liability.

The chain, minute by minute.

The sensor alert at 10:43 fired into the client’s environmental monitoring system, which paged the area supervisor and the on-shift QA engineer. Standard. The client’s runbook for a Grade B excursion requires four pieces of evidence before disposition can proceed:

  1. A continuous particle count log covering the twelve hours preceding the event.
  2. The current-shift cleaning and gowning record, timestamped, with operator identity.
  3. An HVAC performance record covering the last maintenance interval.
  4. A written statement from the area supervisor attributing a probable root cause.

Items 1, 3, and 4 are, in most modern facilities, fast. The sensor log is a query. The HVAC record is a database pull. The supervisor’s statement is a form. Item 2 is the one that usually stalls the investigation. In a typical pharma facility, the cleaning record is a paper checklist in a binder on a cart outside the cleanroom, signed by the gowning operator at the end of shift. Retrieving it during an excursion means walking to the cart, locating the right page, photographing it, uploading it to the QMS, and having a QA reviewer examine it. This process usually takes between two and six hours. During a real excursion, it often takes longer, because the document is incomplete, illegible, or missing.

What took thirteen minutes on the IQS side.

On the IQS side, at 10:44, one minute after the alert fired, the QA engineer opened the customer portal and pulled up cleanroom suite 3. The shift cleaning record, which had been logged into IQS Flow by the sanitation operator at shift change that morning, was visible immediately, with all GPS-and-wrist-worn-sensor-timestamped task evidence. Twelve tasks. All completed. Photos for the three tasks the rubric flagged as evidence-required. Signatures from both the operator and the area supervisor. A sign-off from the environmental-services lead at 06:14 local, roughly four-and-a-half hours before the alert fired.

The QA engineer spent, by her subsequent estimate, thirteen minutes reviewing the evidence. She opened the photos. She checked timestamps against the HVAC record. She noted that the disinfection pattern on the wall-to-floor seam behind the fill line looked complete and fresh. She filed her review into the client’s QMS at 10:57.

13 min Time spent reviewing cleaning-record evidence on the IQS side. The same step, under the previous paper-binder regime, averaged just over four hours.

Root cause, and why it mattered.

At 10:58 the area supervisor filed a root-cause attribution. A contractor had been performing a scheduled HEPA inspection in an adjacent gray-space airlock between 10:25 and 10:42. The door had been opened for a routine re-gown during the inspection. The pressure differential had briefly inverted. A puff of higher-particulate air had migrated into suite 3 through the airlock, producing the sensor spike. The contractor’s airlock log, which was part of their own digital record, confirmed the timing.

This is the kind of root cause that resolves an excursion quickly. No contamination of the batch. No failure of the environmental controls. A procedural mistake in the airlock protocol that had a specific fix (interlock the doors during contractor inspections). The QA engineer’s review of the cleaning record was what allowed this attribution to be credible, because it ruled out the other high-probability cause: inadequate cleaning of suite 3 that morning. If the morning’s cleaning record had been missing, incomplete, or late, the root-cause investigation would have had to proceed as if the cleaning were the potential cause. That would have meant a full re-clean, a re-qualification, and a batch hold.

The re-score.

At 11:05, the IQS inspector crew was dispatched to re-score suite 3 under a drift-recovery rubric. The crew performed a surface-and-corner wipedown survey, captured 24 GPS-timestamped photos, and re-scored the room against the Grade B at-rest rubric. The re-score finished at 11:21. The room passed at 96.8, well inside the client’s acceptance band. The re-score evidence went into the client’s QMS alongside the original cleaning record.

At 11:28 the area supervisor signed the disposition recommendation. At 11:30 QA signed the release. The batch proceeded. The investigation generated a two-page deviation report that referenced seventeen timestamped evidence artifacts, all pulled from a single system, all linked to operator identity and GPS position. The deviation closed the same day.

What we changed afterwards.

A few things came out of this event that have changed how we configure IQS Flow for cleanroom clients. They are small, but worth mentioning.

First, we’ve made the drift-alert handshake first-class in the customer portal. If a client has a connected particle-counter system, an alert can now route directly into IQS Flow and surface the most recent cleaning-record evidence for the affected room to the QA engineer on shift, without the engineer having to go look for it. Four of our cleanroom clients have taken us up on this. It has cut their average excursion-response time from about 3.2 hours to about 52 minutes.

Second, we’ve added a rubric tier for drift-recovery re-scoring, which is what the inspector crew ran during the forty-seven-minute window. This is a narrower rubric than a routine cleanroom inspection. It focuses on the items most relevant to particulate generation: floor-to-wall seams, HEPA grille housings, fill-line shrouds. It runs in about fifteen minutes.

Third, and this is the cultural shift more than the technical one, we stopped describing our cleanroom offering as a “cleaning quality tool.” The QA engineer at the client I described does not care, on the day of a drift event, whether the cleaning was good. She cares whether the cleaning was documented in a way she can rely on to rule it out as a cause. What we sell to cleanroom clients is documentation credibility. The cleaning quality is a prerequisite, but it is not the product.

What this is not.

I want to be careful, as ever, about what this case study claims. It does not claim that IQS Flow prevents drift events. It cannot. Drift is a function of HVAC, procedure, and human behavior; it is not a function of janitorial crews. What IQS Flow does is compress the time between a drift event and a batch disposition by shortening the part of the investigation that is about evidence retrieval. For this client, in this event, that compression was measured in hours of QA time and in about four days of batch hold that did not happen.

The business case is straightforward if you run a pharma facility with a non-trivial drift-event rate. Batch holds cost. A single four-day hold on a high-value biologic can run into seven figures of lost revenue. If your current cleaning record lives in a binder, you are one drift event away from discovering what that binder costs.
