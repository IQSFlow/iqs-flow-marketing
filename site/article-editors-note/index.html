Editor's Note · Vol. 04 Issue 11

We don’t publish vendor press releases. Not even our own.

What “Field Notes” means, why every essay is by-lined by the person who actually stood in the room, and the three things our editorial board rejects on sight.

By Joshua Hinton Apr 12, 2026 3 min read

When we started Field Notes in early 2023, the original working title for the journal was The IQS Flow Blog. We almost shipped with that name. Then I sat down to draft the inaugural post and realized the thing I wanted to publish was nothing like a company blog. It was a long, specific, unflattering piece about why our first product attempt had failed on a regional-hospital pilot. The word “blog” did not describe what that was. We renamed the publication and shipped the piece. A handful of people wrote back. Most of them said the same thing: “I have not read a company publication that admitted a loss in years.”

That correspondence is what the journal is for. Three years later, the rules that grew out of it are worth stating plainly, because we are rejecting more pitches now than we are accepting, and the reasons are consistent.

Rule one: a person was there.

Every piece we publish is by-lined by a person who was physically in the room when the thing happened. Not the person who heard about it, not the person who analyzed it from a spreadsheet, not the VP who approved the case study. The person with the tablet on the ramp. The EVS director who watched the surveyor open the wrong drawer. The station ops lead who got the 3 a.m. phone call. If the author cannot describe what the room smelled like, we do not have a piece yet.

Rule two: a number, or no piece.

Every essay has to turn on a number that we can point to. The 11.3-point gap. The $1.4M in rebates. The 47-minute anomaly-to-disposition cycle. We are not interested in “trends” and we are not interested in “thought leadership.” We are interested in the number a practitioner could use to argue with their counterparty on Monday morning. If the draft does not contain one, we send it back.

Rule three: no press releases.

This is the hardest rule to hold, because the pressure to hold it comes from inside the building. We have rejected twelve internal pitches this year from our own marketing team. We have rejected three from our own sales team. We have, on two occasions, rejected posts about product launches that I wrote myself. The rule is the same for everyone: if the piece reads like a press release, it does not run in Field Notes. It can run in our product release notes. It can run in the newsletter we send to prospects. It cannot run here.

If you are a practitioner who has sat through a JCAHO survey, instrumented a ramp, written a rubric your crew actually followed, or lost an argument with a vendor over a PDF that said 98%, we want your pitch. We pay $1,500 per accepted piece. Send a paragraph describing what happened and a number we could turn a headline on: research@iqsflow.com.

What’s in this issue.

The feature this week is the 11.3-point gap, the largest piece of original research we’ve published since the journal launched. If you only read one thing from Vol. 04, read that. The reported essay on JCAHO surveys is also worth your time; if you run an EVS program, it will change the way you think about survey preparation. And the correspondence from a night-shift lead on rubric cruelty is, in my opinion, the best letter we’ve received this year. It is worth the price of the subscription, which is nothing.

Thank you for reading. We’ll see you in two weeks.

Fin
JH
